# Research and Evidence

Use this reference whenever the analysis depends on current market data, regulations, competitor details, compensation, benchmarks, financing terms, or other externally verifiable claims.

## Source hierarchy

Prefer the closest authoritative source:

1. laws, regulators, government statistics, and official registries;
2. audited filings, company documentation, pricing pages, and investor materials;
3. original research with transparent methodology;
4. reputable industry analysis and reporting;
5. aggregators, vendor posts, and search snippets only as discovery aids.

The best source depends on the claim. A competitor's site is authoritative for its current advertised price, not for its superiority or market share.

## Research workflow

1. Convert the question into an evidence plan: claim, needed measure, geography, segment, date range, and preferred source.
2. Search broadly, then inspect the underlying source rather than citing a result snippet.
3. Record the exact measure, definition, population, publication date, observation period, URL, and relevant caveats.
4. Triangulate material claims with independent methods or sources. Explain conflicts instead of averaging incompatible figures.
5. Normalize currencies, inflation basis, units, periods, and segment definitions before combining data.
6. Mark each value as user-provided, sourced, benchmark, assumption, or calculated output.

## Freshness and citation

Use current research for volatile claims and state the as-of date. Link directly to the supporting page near the claim. Distinguish publication date from the period measured. If only stale, inaccessible, or weak evidence exists, say so and widen the range.

Never fabricate a citation, quote a report not accessed, launder a vendor estimate into an independent fact, or use a paywalled headline without enough methodology to evaluate it.

## Evidence ledger

For source-heavy work, maintain a compact table with: claim or input, value, definition, source, publication/observation date, confidence, and use in the model. This makes later updates and assumption changes auditable.
