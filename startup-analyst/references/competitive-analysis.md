# Competitive Analysis

Use this reference for landscape mapping, alternatives, differentiation, positioning, and competitive response.

## Scope by customer decision

Define the target customer, job to be done, buying context, and geography. Include:

- direct competitors solving the same problem similarly;
- indirect competitors solving it differently;
- substitutes, internal tools, manual workflows, and doing nothing.

Avoid lists built only from category labels; the relevant landscape is the customer's actual choice set.

## Evidence workflow

1. Identify the strategic decision: positioning, segment entry, product priority, pricing, or sales enablement.
2. Select comparison dimensions that influence that decision, such as target segment, use case, workflow, pricing metric, distribution, proof, switching cost, or integration depth.
3. Gather dated evidence from first-party product, pricing, documentation, customer, and regulatory sources. Use third-party sources to supplement, not overwrite, primary evidence.
4. Separate observed facts from interpretations. Mark unavailable pricing or unverified claims as unknown.
5. Compare customer tradeoffs and durable capabilities, not feature-count totals.
6. Test whether proposed differentiation is valuable, provable, difficult to copy, and reachable through the chosen channel.

Use Five Forces, positioning maps, or similar frameworks only when they sharpen the decision.

## Output

Provide:

- market boundary and competitor taxonomy;
- a compact comparison matrix with evidence dates;
- underserved needs or strategic gaps;
- the company's defensible strengths and exposed weaknesses;
- recommended position, supporting proof, and counter-position risks;
- unknowns plus a customer or research plan to resolve them.

Do not infer market share from search visibility, present vendor marketing claims as independent facts, or call a crowded quadrant a “blue ocean” without demand evidence.
