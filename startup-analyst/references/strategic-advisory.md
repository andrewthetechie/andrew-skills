# Strategic Advisory

Use this reference for go-to-market, segment selection, pricing, packaging, partnerships, market entry, and other consequential choices.

## Decision process

1. Frame one decision, deadline, objective, and non-negotiable constraints.
2. Establish the baseline and the cost of doing nothing.
3. Define two to four genuinely distinct options.
4. Choose decision criteria before scoring options. Typical criteria include customer value, evidence strength, speed to learning, economics, strategic fit, reversibility, execution risk, and cash requirement.
5. Show evidence and assumptions behind each score; do not let a weighted matrix disguise unsupported judgment.
6. Identify dependencies, second-order effects, and the failure mode for each option.
7. Recommend an option and translate its weakest assumptions into tests, owners, dates, and decision thresholds.

## Common applications

### Segment and GTM

Compare urgency, willingness to pay, reachable buyer density, sales cycle, retention potential, competition, and the startup's unfair access. Recommend a narrow initial wedge when evidence supports it, plus explicit expansion conditions.

### Pricing and packaging

Separate price metric, package boundaries, price level, and discount policy. Connect the metric to customer value and vendor cost-to-serve. Test willingness to pay and operational feasibility; do not rely only on competitor prices.

### Partnerships

Model partner incentive, customer ownership, distribution reach, integration cost, margin, concentration risk, and time to value. Distinguish a signed partnership from activated distribution and realized revenue.

## Output

Lead with the choice and rationale. Include option tradeoffs, decisive assumptions, economics where relevant, risks, 30/60/90-day actions, validation experiments, and explicit revisit triggers.
